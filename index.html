Build your research question

Answer the six questions below — they'll give you everything you need. Then use what you wrote to craft your overall research question at the bottom.

What does a weak vs. strong question look like?
Too broad — avoid these

"What is immigration policy?"

Just asks what something is — no debate, no action.

"Should schools be better?"

Too vague — "better" could mean anything.

"Is climate change a problem?"

Already settled — not a real policy debate.

Focused & debatable — aim for these

"What should federal policy be on undocumented students in public schools?"

Specific group, specific level, debatable.

"Should Michigan require mental health days be excused absences?"

Specific place, specific policy ask.

"To what extent should Dexter limit cell phones during the school day?"

Local, specific, genuinely debatable.

Answer these six questions first
1. What is the core problem or issue?
In one sentence — what is actually wrong or being debated?
2. Who is most affected by this issue?
A specific group of people — not just "everyone."
3. What level is this issue happening at?
Local, state, national — or a specific place like your school or city?
4. What kind of change do you think is needed?
A new law? A policy change? A program? More funding?
5. Who has the power to make that change?
Congress? A state legislature? A school board? A mayor?
6. What is the strongest argument against your view?
If your question is truly debatable, you should be able to answer this.
↓ Now use your answers above to write your question ↓
Start with "What should…", "How should…", or "To what extent should…" — make sure it's specific enough that someone could disagree.

Choose your format

The best format depends on your audience and your goal. Pick the one that gives your message the best chance of actually being heard.

Reaching elected officials & government

Letter to elected official

A formal letter to your U.S. rep, state legislator, or mayor with a specific policy ask.

Public comment

A short written statement submitted when a government body is seeking input on a proposed rule or policy.

Changing things at school

Letter to the school board

A persuasive letter asking the board to adopt, change, or eliminate a district-level policy.

School operations proposal

A formal proposal to change how something works day-to-day — schedules, procedures, facilities, or school culture.

Unit or lesson proposal

A pitch to a teacher or curriculum team to add, change, or remove content from a specific course.

Student petition

A petition addressed to administration or the board, signed by students, requesting a specific change.

Reaching the public

Op-ed / editorial

A persuasive essay for a newspaper, school publication, or website meant to shift public opinion.

Public service announcement

A short, sharp message — written, visual, or recorded — designed to raise awareness and prompt action.

Social media campaign plan

A strategy for using social platforms to build awareness, shift opinion, or organize people around your issue.

Community forum proposal

A plan to host or organize a public meeting where community members discuss and respond to your issue.

Presenting a plan directly

Policy proposal / memo

A structured document laying out the problem, your recommended solution, how it works, and its costs and benefits.

Speech or presentation

A spoken argument delivered to a real or simulated audience — a board meeting, community event, or class.

Budget or funding proposal

A request for specific funding or resources, showing how money would be spent and what outcomes it would produce.

Resolution or ordinance draft

An attempt to write the actual language of a law, rule, or official resolution the way a governing body would adopt it.
